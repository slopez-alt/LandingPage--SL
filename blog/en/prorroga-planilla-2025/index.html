2025 Puerto Rico Tax Return Extension: what it covers and what it doesn't

Leer en español

Steven López Díaz
Puerto Rico · July 17, 2026
Form SC 2644October 15SURI

If you filed Form SC 2644 before April 15, 2026 to request more time, your new deadline is October 15, 2026. But "more time to file" is not the same as "more time to pay" — and that confusion is what costs taxpayers the most money every year. Here's exactly what your extension protects, and what keeps accruing even if you have one.

The date that already passed and the one coming up

Puerto Rico's Department of the Treasury (Hacienda) set Wednesday, April 15, 2026 as the original deadline to file the 2025 Individual Income Tax Return (Form 482.0), electronically and mandatorily. If you couldn't meet that date, the Extension Request (Form SC 2644) — also electronic and available only through SURI for this tax year — gave you until October 15, 2026 to file the complete return.

Important

That October date does not move. There is no automatic second extension after this one.

What the extension DOES cover

  • Additional time to prepare and file your return. If you were missing a 1099, a K-1, or simply didn't have time to organize your documents, the extension gives you those six extra months without being considered in filing default.
  • Protection from the failure-to-file penalty. As long as you file the complete return before October 15, you avoid the specific penalty for failing to file your return on time.

What the extension does NOT cover

  • Payment of the tax you owe. The extension gives you time to file, not time to pay. If you had a balance due on April 15 and didn't remit it, Hacienda begins accruing interest and surcharges on that balance from that date — whether or not your six-month extension is running.
  • The "relief check" if you didn't qualify on time. As announced by the Department this cycle, the additional payment for taxpayers with net taxable income of $150,000 or less requires having filed the return or the extension within the established period (that is, on or before April 15), having resided in Puerto Rico for all of 2025, and not having elected the optional tax or being subject to the alternate basic tax. If your extension was filed late, this benefit is out of reach regardless of when you finish filing the return.
  • A second extension. There is no extension on top of the extension for individuals. October 15 is final.

If you still owe money: what to do before October

  1. Don't wait until October 15 to pay what you can. Every month that passes since April, the outstanding balance accrues interest and surcharges — paying something now reduces that accumulation even if you're not filing the complete return yet.
  2. Check the status of your Extension Request in SURI. Confirm it was accepted, not just submitted; an incomplete submission does not protect you from the failure-to-file penalty.
  3. If you'll owe and can't pay all at once, ask about a payment plan before the extension expires, not after. A plan requested proactively is handled differently than a debt already in collections.
  4. Gather now the documents you were missing in April — 480 informative returns, 1099s, K-1s, withholding evidence — so you don't reach October with the same rush.

Frequently asked questions

Does the extension protect me from interest if I owe tax?+

No. The extension only protects you from the failure-to-file penalty. Interest and surcharges on any unpaid tax balance accrue from the original due date (April 15), regardless of the extension.

Can I request another extension after October 15?+

No. For individuals there is no second extension after the six-month period.

What if I never filed the Extension Request and April 15 has passed?+

You are outside the extension period and the failure-to-file penalty may apply from April. The sooner you file the return, the lower your exposure — contact us to evaluate your specific case.

Does the extension work the same for individual and corporate returns?+

The mechanism (Form SC 2644, electronic-only through SURI) is the same form, but each taxpayer type has its own original due date and its own applicable Circular Letter (CC RI 26-02 for individuals, CC RI 26-06 for corporations). If you have both types of returns, don't assume they share a deadline.
